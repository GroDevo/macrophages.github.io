What is a Bacteriophage?

Key Takeaways:

Bacteriophages are certain types of viruses whose main victims are various types of bacteria.

Categories of Bacteriophages
All viruses are characterized by their order, family, and subfamily. One of the most common types of bacteriophages, T4 species, belongs to the Caudovirales order, of the Myoviridae family, and of the Tevenvirinae subfamily. Each category (order, family, subfamily) specifies distinctive viruses with distinctive capabilities.
Genetic Makeup
Bacteriophages have a very recognizable form since it looks different from what people have traditionally known as a virus (i.e. a round thing with spikes). Though, viruses are essentially simple. The bacteriophage contains its genetic information inside its protein capsid, which is the fairly round shape above its tail fibre (or what seems like legs). The genetic information is either DNA or RNA.
Replication and Infection Cycle
Just like other viruses, bacteriophages require a host to reproduce. Some phages are capable of 1 or 2 cycles. The Lytic cycle and/or the Lysogenic cycle.
  • The Lytic Cycle (Kills the Host)
    1. The bacteriophage attaches to the host
    2. It injects its own DNA genome
    3. DNA and protein (the morph of the virus) are replicated
    4. DNA and protein combine to make a new phage
    5. New phages use proteins to create exit holes, allowing water to enter, and ultimately killing the host
  • The Lysogenic Cycle (Does Not Kill the Host)
    1. The phage attaches to the host
    2. The DNA injected combines with the bacteria's chromosomes and is deactivated
    3. Prophage (the integrated DNA) will now be copied when the bacteria mulitplies
